About

Not a researcher.
Not a developer.
A practitioner.

I'm an AI hobbyist operating at the intersection of interior design, financial tools, and spatial planning. My interest isn't in AI as abstraction — it's in what happens when you put it inside a real workflow and see what breaks, improves, or surprises you.

The artifacts I build are working prototypes, not polished products. They're experiments in the most practical sense: real inputs, real outputs, real constraints. I'm particularly drawn to domains where humans still make consequential decisions manually — and where AI can compress the cognitive overhead without removing the judgment.

Everything here is built in Singapore, in my own time, with no institutional agenda. Exploratory, not promotional.

Key findings

What I've learned
by building, not reading.

Observations that came from actually using AI inside design and planning workflows — not from benchmark papers.

01
AI value scales with organisational altitude
Directors get the highest ROI from AI — cross-studio visibility is impossible for a single human. Designers benefit modestly. The pattern holds across every domain I've tested: the higher the coordination burden, the more AI earns its place.
← From: AI Studio Intelligence by Role
02
90% drafted, 10% decided — is the right ratio
The SketchUp pipeline shows this clearly: AI handles geometry, annotation, and zone detection; the human reviews gaps and signs off. Trying to push AI past 90% collapses quality faster than it saves time.
← From: SketchUp Detail Drawing Pipeline
03
Compounding beats timing — always
The S&P tracker makes this visceral: at 10.5% returns, SGD 15,000 plus SGD 200/month becomes over SGD 1.1M at age 55. The math is unforgiving in both directions — starting early matters far more than optimising the rate.
← From: S&P 500 Investment Tracker
04
Circulation is the hidden cost of bad planning
The office planner reveals that every 1% increase in circulation allocation adds square metres that cost money but generate nothing. Most studios don't track this — making the tool's efficiency score more actionable than any area spreadsheet.
← From: Office Space Planner
Process

How these
get made.

A rough log of the thinking, iteration, and dead-ends behind each artifact.

STAGE 01
Domain immersion — find the real friction
Every artifact starts with a domain where humans are doing something expensive with their cognition. For the studio dashboard: tracking project health across 6+ concurrent projects and 8 people is genuinely impossible without a tool. That's the entry point.
Research Problem definition
STAGE 02
Prototype in HTML first, think in data second
I build the UI before I know what the data model is. This sounds backwards but it forces the interface to be grounded in what a human actually needs to see — not what's easy to store. The office planner went through four different room categorisation schemes before settling on five.
Prototyping Iteration
STAGE 03
Layer AI where the decision is repetitive, not where it's consequential
AI flags overloaded staff — it doesn't reassign them. AI drafts spec clauses — it doesn't approve them. This separation is deliberate. Every AI layer I build has a clear "human takes over here" boundary. The SketchUp pipeline has two: zone review and annotation sign-off.
AI integration Human-AI handoff
STAGE 04
Test with a real user, not a friendly one
The hospital space planner was stress-tested by someone with no context — they just opened it and tried to plan a ward. Three things broke immediately. None of them would have been caught in self-testing. This is the step I keep learning to do earlier.
User testing Failure analysis
